Dua tambah dua sama dengan lima

6 November, 2025

Baru aja selesai baca 1984 karya George Orwell, buku yang sebenernya aku pinjem secara nggak sengaja dari temen kerja. Waktu itu cuma nyebut judulnya doang, eh ternyata dia punya bukunya. Awalnya nggak ada niat kuat buat baca, tapi karena bukunya udah di tangan, ya sekalian aja. Proses bacanya makan waktu hampir tiga minggu.

Sebelum baca, sering denger orang bilang 1984 itu klasik wajib baca dengan pesan mendalam soal masa depan. Ekspektasiku lumayan tinggi. Kenyataannya, buku ini banyak banget ngejelasin latar dunia, sistem, dan aturan-aturan di dalamnya secara detail. Jujur aja, di beberapa bagian agak bingung sama urgensi tokoh utamanya, dan pacing-nya kerasa lambat. tipe buku yang lebih ke maksa buat mikir.

Tapi setelah idenya mulai nyantol, di situ kekuatan 1984 kerasa banget. Buku ini lebih mirip ramalan daripada fiksi. Konsep kayak manipulasi kebenaran, pengawasan terus-menerus, dan doublethink digambarin sebagai sesuatu yang harus dilawan, tapi ironisnya banyak yang terasa familiar. Kalau dibaca dari sudut pandang negara dunia ketiga—termasuk tempat ku tinggal—beberapa hal ekstrem di novel ini justru udah kejadian, cuma dibungkus dengan istilah yang beda.

Itulah yang membuat 1984 tetap relevan sampai sekarang. Orwell mungkin nggak meramalkan masa depan secara tepat, tapi ia paham gimana kekuasaan dan manusia bekerja. Bukunya memang tegas—peringatannya jelas: kalau orang berhenti berpikir kritis, realitas bisa dikendalikan sesuka hati. Meski berat dan kadang membuat nggak nyaman, 1984 tetap layak dibaca.

Two plus two equals five

November 6, 2025

Just finished reading 1984 by George Orwell, a book I randomly ended up borrowing from a coworker after casually mentioning it in conversation. I didn’t even plan to read it at first, but once I had it in my hands, it felt like one of those books you’re supposed to read at least once in your life. It took me almost three weeks to finish.

Before reading it, I’d heard people constantly calling 1984 a “must-read classic,” full of deep warnings about the future. Going in, I expected some kind of mind-blowing revelation. Instead, the novel spends a lot of time explaining its world, its systems, and its background in extreme detail. At times, I honestly struggled to understand the urgency of the main character’s actions, and the pacing felt slow. It’s tries to corner you into thinking.

But once the ideas settle, that’s where the book hits hard. 1984 feels less like fiction and more like a long, uncomfortable prophecy. Concepts like doublethink, manipulated truth, and constant surveillance are presented as things we should resist, yet they feel oddly familiar. Reading it from a third-world country perspective makes the experience even stranger—some of the “extreme” ideas in the novel already exist in everyday life, just normalized and renamed.

That’s what makes 1984 still relevant today. Orwell may not have predicted the future exactly, but he understood how power and people work. The book is straightforward—its warning is clear: when people stop thinking critically, reality can be shaped at will. Though heavy and sometimes uncomfortable, 1984 is still worth reading.
